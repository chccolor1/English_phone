# A MUSHROOM AND THE MIND

The important thing is not to stop questioning.
Albert Einstein

In some places in Asia, people eat wild mushrooms as part of everyday food. Most are safe when they are cooked well. But one mushroom has become very famous because it can cause a very strange effect. Some people who eat it report seeing many tiny people, like little humans moving around the room.

This mushroom is called Lanmaoa asiatica. Scientists have only recently described it in detail, so it is still new to science. It has been reported in China and the Philippines, and researchers are also looking into similar stories from Papua New Guinea. Even in different places, the reports are very similar. People often describe the same kind of vision: small human-like figures walking, climbing, or standing on objects.

In Yunnan Province in China, this mushroom is known by local people and is even sold in markets during mushroom season. It is used as food, but people say it must be cooked carefully. If it is not cooked enough, it may cause hallucinations. This is not just a local story anymore. Researchers are now studying it in laboratories to understand what is happening.

One reason scientists are interested is the consistency of the hallucinations. Many psychoactive substances can cause different experiences in different people. But with L. asiatica, the "tiny people" effect appears again and again in many reports. That makes this mushroom unusual and important for brain research.

The story of this mushroom also shows something bigger: nature still has many secrets. Scientists believe that a large number of fungal species around the world are still unknown or poorly studied. Each one may contain unique chemicals with risks, benefits, or medical value.

Researchers are still trying to find the exact chemical compound that causes these visions. So far, early tests suggest it may not be the same as the well-known compounds in "magic mushrooms," such as psilocybin. The effect also seems very different. Reports suggest the symptoms can last a long time, and in some cases, people need hospital care. Because of this, scientists are treating the topic carefully and focusing on safety.

For now, Lanmaoa asiatica is both a warning and a scientific mystery. It reminds us that food, culture, and science can connect in surprising ways—and that even a small mushroom can open big questions about the human mind.

This research is important for another reason. Some people experience "illiputian hallucinations" even when they have not eaten mushrooms. These cases are rare, but they can be serious. By studying Lanmaoa asiatica, scientists may learn more about how the brain creates these visual experiences. In the future, this could help doctors better understand certain neurological conditions and possibly improve treatment.
