# RACING PAST THE NEXT BREAKTHROUGH

The important thing is not to stop questioning.

In April 2026, artificial intelligence looks stronger than ever. New models improve quickly, companies invest billions, and AI tools are now part of everyday work. Stanford's 2025 AI Index shows fast progress in benchmarks, rising business use, and very large private investment, especially in the United States.

But there is a growing concern behind this success: the race is becoming so intense that it may be slowing the next big breakthrough.

The problem is not a lack of talent or money. It is pressure. In today's AI industry, researchers often face pressure from investors, deadlines, product launches, and the need to publish quickly. In Llion Jones's TED talk, he argues that this environment can narrow research instead of expanding it. His point is simple: when people feel they must produce results fast, they often choose safer ideas instead of bold ones.

This creates a pattern of "small improvements" on the same systems. These improvements are useful, but they can also trap the field. Teams spend time tuning existing architectures, chasing benchmark gains, and copying what competitors are doing, because these are easier to defend in a crowded market. That may help in the short term, but it can reduce the chance of discovering something truly new.

There are signs of this pressure across the research ecosystem. AI conferences are struggling with scale. An ICLR 2025 conference post noted very large year-over-year submission growth, which can make review quality harder to maintain. NeurIPS 2025 also reported 21,575 valid submissions to the main track, showing how overloaded the system has become. When researchers and reviewers are overwhelmed, careful, slower, more experimental work can lose attention.

Competition also affects openness. The 2025 Foundation Model Transparency Index found that average transparency across major AI companies was low, with an average score of 40.69/100, and especially weak disclosure on training data and compute. If companies share less, others cannot learn from failures, compare methods fairly, or build on each other's work efficiently.

So, what should change?

AI needs a better balance between exploitation (improving what already works) and exploration (testing new ideas that may fail). That means giving some researchers more time, more autonomy, and fewer short-term targets. It also means rewarding originality, not only speed.

Managers and investors can help by protecting a portion of research budgets for high-risk projects. Conferences can continue improving review systems so quality does not collapse under volume. And companies can share more technical details when possible, especially for safety and evaluation methods. AI is moving fast. That is good. But if the field only runs faster in the same direction, it may miss the next leap. Real breakthroughs often begin in quieter spaces: with freedom, curiosity, and enough time to try an idea that does not look obvious yet.
