# FUNDING THE ENERGY SHIFT

Perfect is the enemy of good.

In April 2026, many people agree on one thing: the world needs more clean energy. We need more solar power, wind power, batteries, and stronger electricity grids. The good news is that investment is growing. The International Energy Agency (IEA) said global energy investment in 2025 was expected to reach about USD 3.3 trillion, and around USD 2.2 trillion of that was for cleaner energy areas such as renewables, grids, storage, and electrification.

This sounds like progress. But there is still a big problem.

A lot of climate money does not reach the places and companies that need help to change. Many investors prefer projects that already look "fully green." They feel safer funding a new solar farm than helping a coal-heavy power company change step by step. This is understandable, but it can slow the real energy transition.

Today, experts use the term transition finance for money that helps high-emission sectors move toward lower emissions over time. The IEA explains that this kind of finance is important for emissions-intensive countries, companies, and sectors, especially where clean technologies are not yet easy or cheap to use.

Why does this matter? Because the world does not run only on "easy" projects. Heavy industries like steel, cement, and transport are part of daily life. If these sectors cannot get support to modernize, they may stay the same for many more years. That means more emissions, more pollution, and slower progress.

Another challenge is fairness. Investment is still concentrated in advanced economies and China, while many emerging and developing countries receive much less, even though their energy needs are growing fast. IRENA notes that energy transition investment reached a record level in 2024, but most countries are still being left behind. CPI also highlights that finance for emerging markets and developing economies is key because these countries represent most of the world's population and are growing quickly.

So what should change?

First, climate finance should focus on credible transition plans, not only perfect labels. If a company has a clear plan to cut emissions, add renewables, and report progress, that plan should be considered seriously.

Second, trust is important. Countries and businesses need finance that is faster, simpler, and more realistic. If funding comes with too many delays or conditions, projects may stop before they begin.

Third, accountability must stay strong. Supporting transition finance does not mean giving a free pass. It means funding change with clear targets, timelines, and public reporting.

The energy transition is not a school test where only perfect answers count. It is a real-world process. If we want cleaner power systems in time, climate finance must support both clean solutions and difficult transitions. Progress, step by step, can still lead to a cleaner future.
